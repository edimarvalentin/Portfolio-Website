¡Hola!

I’m Edimar Valentín Kery, an undergraduate student of The Computer Science and Engineering Department at the University of Puerto Rico, Mayagüez Campus. I’m a hands-on developer with experience in many areas where coding is required. I have worked with robotics, video-game development, mobile, desktop and web applications. I even tackled data science at a certain point.

My career in computer science began way before I set foot on college. My introduction to code was merely a coincidence, no one in my family was good with computers and only my mother had higher level education. When I was a young teen, I just liked to play games on my mom’s computer. I wanted to be a commercial pilot at the time, so I had no curiosity of the inner workings of the computer I was using and just assumed the electrons inside were smart. Two games I liked to play eventually invited my curiosity. The games ‘Minecraft’ and ‘Roblox’ invited their players to be creative and make their own things and one day I caved. I bravely went to the documentation pages to discover that if you want to make your own custom game objects, you had to learn this thing called ‘Lua’. I started reading and within minutes I had printed my first ‘Hello, World!’ in the console.

When I got into high school, I enrolled into the school’s vocational program where I took electronics. My teachers were ex-alumni on the university I am enrolled as of now and they knew a lot about computers. During my time there, I learned how simple logic circuits make up the modern-day computer and the things I learned prior to that came together to supplement what I was learning. That’s when I knew where I was headed and what type of work I will do.

Projects

Hippo

A task management Android app that helps the user to organize what they need to do, while giving the user the ability to make timed to do lists and set reminders. It was developed in Android Studio in a team of 3 alumni of the University of Puerto Rico. Git had to be used to implement the tasks of each developer and maintain version control. A backend and parse service were setup to save and retrieve user data.

Mini Sumo Combat Robot

Assembled and programmed the team’s first ever mini sumo robot which is controlled by an Arduino Leonardo microcontroller. The sumo has infrared sensors all around it and 2 motors that give it linear and rotational movement. The mini sumo is controlled by an Arduino Leonardo Microcontroller. The programs are developed in the Arduino IDE and the programming language is practically C++. With a series of sensors embedded in the robot, events can be setup so that the robot can act.

RUMblebots Control PCB V1

After finishing the first mini sumo, a major update had to be made. The Arduino Leonardo used had a limiting number of I/O terminals, meaning that not many sensors could be installed on the robot. With my experience in electronics, I came up with a shield that allows us to use a Teensy microcontroller instead, increasing the number of I/0 terminals from 5 to 12.
